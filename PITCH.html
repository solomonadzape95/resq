The Problem

Nigeria's emergency system is broken.

Three failures stack into one outcome. Help that doesn't arrive in time.

01

112 doesn't pick up

The national emergency line exists on paper. In practice, call drop rates are high, dispatch is manual, and most LGAs have no integration.

02

Most phones can't run an app

Feature phones, prepaid SIMs, no data credit in the moment of crisis. An app-first emergency line only serves the wealthy.

03

Responders exist, invisibly

Off-duty doctors, nurses, NYSC corps members, fire wardens, vigilantes. The supply is already there. The system to find them is not.

What ResQ Is

ResQ is not an app.

It is the matching layer between citizens in crisis and the trained responders already nearby, made visible to government in real time.

The Citizen

Calls from any phone. Knows nothing about how it works.

The Responder

Off-duty professional already on the street. One tap to accept.

The Coordinator

State or LGA dispatcher. Watches every incident live. Audits every outcome.

Help is on the way

The system locates the caller, extracts the situation, and dispatches the nearest verified responders.

Location comes from the call itself. The telco hands us an approximate fix the moment the line connects (cell-tower triangulation), then the AI tightens it from what the caller says. The caller never has to read out coordinates or know where they are.

What the AI Pulls Out

The caller talks. The system listens for three things.

01

Severity

A 1 to 10 triage score, the type of emergency, and how many responders to send.

02

Location

"Behind the big mosque on Aba Road" becomes a geocoded pin on the live map within seconds.

03

Urgency signals

Words like "bleeding" or "not breathing" raise the severity and re-rank the dispatch.

My dad, 67, chest pain, we are near Shoprite Lekki Phase 1.

Pin lands at Shoprite. Severity high. Two responders ranked by distance. No human typed a word.
